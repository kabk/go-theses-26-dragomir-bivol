Shanzai

Copying, besides being a tool for unity and cultural development, can act as a form of creativity within installed monopolies. In the same way as church operates in the monopoly of knowledge through printing the books and sharing literacy, todays alternative in form of brands, dominate the global market, dictating the price products and luxury items. If marketplace have always been piles of objects in a display of richness, today’s supermarket and malls are different. They represent galleries or cathedrals of copying with their endless lanes and canyons stacked with vast quantities of goods. This is the other side of copia as abundance that Taussig Michael Taussig. Mimesis and AlterityA Particular History of the Senses, 1993 calls “the organization of mimesis” through the global capitalist economy, the nation-state and its various appendages and substrates.

This discussions surrounding reproduction become tangible when viewed through everyday experiences. Growing up in Moldova, the original was a scarcity and most of the goods one could afford were cheap counterfeits coming from China or Turkey. The presence of imitation products shaped how authenticity and value were perceived in daily life. “Made in China” inscription was an understatement, while “designed in California” was a symbol of social status. I had a little understanding what the original looks like. You could never trust the seller, being so naïve and unexperienced. You could maybe spot the weird stitching, or a visible line of glue, that gave it a saddle yet prominent cheap look. Noone would ask, nor criticize. My first pair of adidas was fake SPECIALZES that I wore for year. Blue suede with brown sole, subtle completion of my look. Vividly remember the piles of clothes, rows of shoes and displays of devices with shiny LEDs and green price tags. Never I would ask myself whether the original is better, I always knew, it is.

During the early-2000s, early instances of shanzai production began, mostly simple counterfeiting of electronic goods including DVD and MP3 players. In the mid-2000s, more advanced products such as smartphones were being created, with varying levels of skill and quality, leading to the term shnazai being applied to counterfeit goods. By the end of 2006, it is estimated that shanzai mobile phone manufacturers accounted of around 30 percent of the domestic phone market in China.

It becomes a cultural phenomenon that overtook the global market. It is related to genius reappropriation, often, making the copy superior to the original. It is through close study of the original, and understanding the building elements of it, a copy can find its identity and authority. This object study, becomes often an ironic imitation, for example a phone that has an extra functionality like an extra radio antenna, or two SD slots with chunky batteries, In this way, it establishes itself as an original, illustrating a particular type of creativity. Gradually departing from the original, mutating into something unexpected. Labels become “dadaist game”, a playground for combinations like: ADIDOS, ADADA, ADADIS, ADIS, DASIDA, the list goes on. Counterfeiting exposes the fragility of surface value, pushing brands to invest in what cannot be imitate: the craft. Craftstmanship, rooted in skill and artisty, becomes a weapon against replication. Brands aim for handcraft and value imperfection over mechanization. Productions on the local level can never be brought to perfection; every manufectured “artefact” tells the story of their origin and values, brands fortify thus their identity turnign history against forgery. The inevitable loss of aura, as mentioned by Walter Benjamin in essay ‘The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction” publiched in 1935: “It might be stated as a general formula that the technology of reproduction detaches the reproduced object from the sphere of tradition. By replicating the work many times over, it substitutes a mass existence”. Yet, while diminishing the uniqueness of traditional art, there is potential to democratization that is made accessible to tehh pubic. The function of art is shifted from ritual to politics, enabling a more participatory and politically engaged relationship with art.

The statement ties strongly with debated about forged goods and accessories. Shanzai products, can create a new kind of aura rooted in accessibility, shared experience and cultural relevance. For example, counterfeits circulate outside of official markets, creating a sense of subcultural authenticity or resitance to corporate control. This new form of aura is not tied to the quality of the object or its institutional context, but emerges from the ways in which the reproduced item is shared, reinterpreted and integrated into everyday life. In this sense, the aura or “authenticity” of pritaed media lies in its aility to connect people, challenge power structures, and create new cultural meaning, even as it undermines the traditional aura or authority of the original work.
